Step 3: Dissect Your Degree

This is the core process. You're going to take your degree requirements apart, course by course, and find the cheapest and fastest way to earn each credit. Here's exactly how to do it:

3A. Pull Your Degree Requirements

Go to your target university's website. Search for "[your major] degree requirements" or "[your major] degree plan." You're looking for the official course catalog listing that shows every course you need to graduate. It typically breaks down into three buckets:

  • General Education (Gen-Ed): 30-45 credits. English, math, science, humanities, social sciences. This is where credit stacking has the highest impact. Nearly every gen-ed course has a CLEP, AP, or DSST equivalent.
  • Major Requirements: 30-60 credits. Courses specific to your major. Some lower-level ones have exam equivalents; upper-level ones usually must be taken at the university.
  • Electives: 15-30 credits. Free choice. Almost all of these can be satisfied by exams.

3B. Cross-Reference Each Course

For each course requirement, ask: "Can I earn this credit by exam instead of paying tuition?" Use the reference table below to find the match. Then verify it on Transferology.

Step 4: Verify on Transferology

The table above shows the general equivalents. But every school has its own policies. Here's how to verify:

  1. Log into your Transferology account
  2. Select "Will My Courses Transfer?"
  3. Add each exam you plan to take (CLEP, AP, DSST)
  4. Add your target school as the destination
  5. Hit "Search for Matches" — you'll see exactly which credits your school accepts and what they count as
  6. Update your spreadsheet's "Transfer Verified?" column to "Yes" for each confirmed match
🏫
Watch Your School's Credit Cap
Many universities limit how many credits you can transfer via exam. Common caps:

  • 30 credits — selective schools like UMich, UVA
  • 45 credits — many state universities
  • 60 credits — transfer-friendly schools like ASU, Penn State
  • 90 credits — WGU, TESU, Excelsior (designed for credit stackers)

Exceeding the cap means wasted exams. Check your school's limit BEFORE building your attack sequence.

Step 5: Prioritize by ROI

Not all credits are created equal. Prioritize exams in this order:

  1. Lowest-cost exams first: Anything you can get a Modern States voucher for = exam fee reimbursed (only ~$20-35 sitting fee)
  2. Highest credit value: Spanish Language and Natural Sciences give 6 credits each — double the value of a single exam
  3. Easiest exams: American Government, Introductory Psychology, Introductory Sociology have the highest pass rates
  4. Gen-ed requirements: These are universal — they transfer to nearly every school and satisfy common requirements
  5. Gap-fillers last: Use DSST for subjects that CLEP doesn't cover (Public Speaking, Business Ethics, Personal Finance)
📋 Transfer Eligibility — Real Examples

Credits that typically TRANSFER at 2,900+ schools:

  • CLEP Principles of Macroeconomics → counts as ECON 101 / Intro to Economics (3 credits)
  • CLEP Introductory Psychology → counts as PSYC 101 / General Psychology (3 credits)
  • CLEP American Government → counts as POLS 101 / American Politics (3 credits)
  • CLEP College Algebra → counts as MATH 110 / College Algebra (3 credits)
  • CLEP Spanish Level 2 → counts as SPAN 201 + 202 (6-12 credits depending on score level and institution)
  • CLEP Western Civilization I & II → satisfies History gen-ed (6 credits total)
  • AP Calculus AB (score 3+) → counts as CALC 1 (4 credits)
  • AP Statistics (score 3+) → counts as STAT 201 (3 credits)
  • DSST Personal Finance → counts as FIN 101 / Personal Finance elective (3 credits)
  • DSST Principles of Statistics → counts as STAT 200 at many schools (3 credits)

Credits that often DON'T transfer (school-specific or major-restricted):

  • Upper-division major requirements — e.g., a nursing clinical or engineering lab course cannot be replaced by a CLEP exam
  • Capstone / thesis courses — schools require these to be completed in-residence; no exam equivalent exists
  • Writing-intensive requirements at selective schools — some schools (Boston University, Northeastern) require in-house composition courses
  • CLEP scores below 50 — the passing threshold; anything below doesn't count for credit anywhere
  • Exams at schools with no CLEP policy — always verify. A small number of schools don't accept any CLEP. Check before you prep.
  • Duplicate credit — if you already took Psych 101 at your current school, CLEP Introductory Psychology won't give you additional credit
  • Health/PE requirements — most schools require these to be fulfilled in-person; no CLEP or DSST covers them

Rule of thumb: gen-ed requirements transfer almost everywhere. Major-specific upper-division courses rarely do. Use Transferology to verify every single credit before you study for the exam.

⚠️
The 6 Most Expensive Mistakes
  1. Studying for an exam your school doesn't accept — always verify on Transferology AND call the registrar BEFORE you study. Never assume.
  2. Taking both CLEP Psychology AND AP Psychology — these are duplicate credits; your school will only count one. Pick whichever you score higher on, not both.
  3. Exceeding your school's transfer credit cap — many schools cap exam credits at 30–60 (e.g., University of Michigan caps CLEP at 30 credits). Exams beyond the cap earn you nothing.
  4. Prepping for upper-division exams that rarely transfer — focus on gen-ed equivalents first. Upper-division credit-by-exam has low acceptance rates and high difficulty.
  5. Not documenting your verification — if a registrar says "yes we accept that," get it in email. Staff turnover means verbal promises can evaporate. A paper trail protects you.
  6. Forgetting transcript sending fees — each CLEP score report costs $20 to send. If you're sending to 3 schools, that's $60 per exam on top of everything else. Budget for this upfront.
🔄
Advanced: The "Credit Laundering" Technique
Some universities don't accept CLEP directly — but they do accept transfer courses from community colleges. Here's the workaround:

Step 1: Pass a CLEP exam (e.g., Introductory Psychology).
Step 2: Send your CLEP score to a community college that grants credit for it (most do).
Step 3: The community college converts it into a course on your transcript (e.g., "PSY 101 — B").
Step 4: Transfer that course (not the exam) to your target university. They see a community college transcript, not a CLEP score.

This works because universities that reject "credit by exam" will still accept "transfer credit" from accredited schools. The credit is the same — the packaging is different.

Texas students: This technique can also achieve "Core Complete" status, which guarantees your general education credits transfer to any Texas public university. Search "CLEP Core Complete Texas" for details.

Note: This is widely used in the accelerated-degree community (DegreeForum.net, HS4CC). Check that your community college grants CLEP credit before sending scores.

Step 6: Build Your Attack Sequence

Now schedule your exams. One per month is sustainable. One every 2-3 weeks is aggressive but doable with the study system in Chapter 8. Map it out:

🤖 AI Prompt — Degree Dissection Assistant
I'm a [grade level / situation] planning to attend [university name] for a [major name] degree. The degree requires [X] total credits. Here are my degree requirements: [paste course list from university website]. For each course, tell me: (1) Is there a CLEP, AP, or DSST exam that matches this course? (2) What's the exam name, cost, and difficulty? (3) Does [university name] accept this exam for credit? (4) What's the recommended study time? Organize the results into a prioritized list, starting with the easiest and highest-ROI exams first.
Important: AI tools cannot reliably verify school-specific transfer policies. Use AI for identifying exam equivalents, estimating difficulty, and building study plans. For actual transfer verification, ALWAYS use Transferology and call your registrar directly. AI will hallucinate transfer policies.

Dissect Your Actual Degree
