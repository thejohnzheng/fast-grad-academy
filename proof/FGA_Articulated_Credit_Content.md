# Earn College Credit While Still in High School

## What Is Articulated College Credit?

Many high schools have agreements with local colleges and technical schools that let you earn **real college credit** for courses you're already taking. This means you could walk into college with credits already on your transcript — before you even graduate high school.

This is called **Articulated College Credit**, and it's one of the most overlooked shortcuts in education.

## How It Works

1. **You complete qualifying high school courses** — subjects like Accounting, Business Management, Digital Photography, and more can count.
2. **Your high school has agreements with specific colleges** — each agreement maps your high school course to an equivalent college course.
3. **You request your credits be sent to the college** — once accepted, those credits appear on your college transcript just like any other course.

For example, a single Accounting course at Champlin Park High School qualified for credit at **7 different colleges** — including Anoka Technical College, Central Lakes College, Hennepin Technical College, and Minnesota State College Southeast. One class, multiple credit options.

## The Minimum Viable Score: Stop Overstudy, Start Stacking

Here's something most students get wrong: they think they need to ace these exams. You don't.

Every credit-by-exam program has a **minimum passing score** — hit that number and you get the same credit as someone who scored 20 points higher. The transcript just says "credit earned." Nobody asks what you scored.

**What the minimums actually look like:**

- **CLEP exams:** Most colleges accept a score of **50** (out of 80). Some require higher for specific subjects — always check with your target school.
- **DSST exams:** The standard passing score is **400** (out of 500). That's it. A 400 and a 480 earn the same credit.
- **AP exams:** Most colleges accept a **3** (out of 5). Some competitive schools want a 4 or 5 for certain subjects, but a 3 gets you credit at the majority of institutions.

**Why this matters:** You don't need to spend 3 months mastering a subject. You need to spend enough time to clear the bar, earn the credit, and move on to the next one.

The goal isn't perfection — it's **throughput**. A student who passes 6 exams with minimum scores earns more credit than a student who gets a perfect score on 2. Time is the resource you're optimizing, not your GPA on exams that don't even show up as letter grades.

**One important caveat:** Always check the specific score your target institution requires. Some schools set their own minimums higher than the standard. Verify before you sit for the exam so you know exactly what number you're aiming for.

## Why This Matters for Fast Graduation

Every articulated credit you claim is one less class you need to take (and pay for) in college. Combined with CLEP and DSST exams, articulated credit can shave entire semesters off your degree timeline.

Think about it:
- **AP exams** test you on college-level material you studied in class
- **CLEP/DSST exams** let you test out of courses on your own schedule
- **Articulated credit** gives you college credit for courses you've *already completed*

Stack all three, and you're walking into college with a serious head start.

## Important: Always Verify With Your School

Before pursuing articulated credit, **contact both your high school counselor and the college you plan to attend.** Articulation agreements vary by state, school district, and institution. They can also change from year to year.

Here's what to confirm:
- **Does your high school participate?** Not all schools have articulation agreements.
- **Which courses qualify?** The specific courses that count depend on the agreement between your high school and the college.
- **Is the agreement still active?** Agreements can expire or be updated — verify that the one you're relying on is current.
- **What's the process to claim credit?** Each college has its own process for accepting articulated credit. Some require you to submit paperwork before a deadline.
- **Will the credits transfer?** If you plan to transfer from a community college to a university, make sure the articulated credits will follow you.

**Bottom line:** Articulated credit is a powerful tool, but don't assume — always verify directly with the institutions involved before building your plan around it.

## Take Action

1. Talk to your high school counselor about which courses have articulation agreements
2. Identify the colleges those agreements connect to
3. Map those credits against your degree requirements
4. Combine with CLEP/DSST exam credits to maximize your head start

The students who graduate fastest aren't necessarily the smartest — they're the ones who know which credits count and how to stack them.
