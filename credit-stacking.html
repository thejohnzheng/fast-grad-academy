Core How-To Section

Credit stacking, step by step.

This is the operating manual: pull the degree map, translate every requirement into a cheaper credit source, verify transfer before spending money, then stack credits in the order that keeps the finish line open.

Start the Map
6 moves Map degree requirements, choose credit sources, match exams, verify with the registrar, sequence the stack, and print the checklist.

01
Find Your Degree Map

Start with the finish line.

Do not begin by asking, "Which CLEP should I take?" Begin by asking, "What exact credits does my target degree require?" The degree map is the master blueprint. Every cheap credit is only useful if it lands inside that blueprint.

Where to find it

Search your school site for the degree name plus one of these phrases: degree requirements, program plan, catalog year, academic plan, or degree audit. Save the PDF or webpage before you make any exam decisions.

  • Confirm the catalog year you are using.
  • Separate general education, major, elective, residency, and capstone rules.
  • Highlight every requirement that says "must be completed at this institution."
Thomas Edison State University Catalog → Excelsior University Academic Policies → Charter Oak State College Catalog →

Annotated sample degree audit

When you look at an audit, treat it like a puzzle board. You are not trying to be clever; you are trying to identify which boxes can be filled externally and which boxes are locked.

Written Communication 3 cr Can often be satisfied through CLEP College Composition or transfer English Comp.
Social Sciences 6 cr Use CLEP Psychology, Sociology, or History if the school accepts those exams.
Capstone 3 cr Usually must be taken at the degree-granting school. Do not try to replace it.
John's rule: if a credit does not map to a requirement, it is not progress yet. It might still be useful as an elective, but you do not count it until you know exactly where it lands.
02
Know Your Options

Every requirement has a credit source.

Credit stacking works because college credit can come from more than one place. Your job is to choose the cheapest valid source for each requirement without creating transfer problems later.

CLEP

Best first stop for general education: psychology, sociology, history, composition, college math, business, and language credits. Modern States may cover the exam fee if you complete the free course.

CLEP Exam List → Modern States →

DSST

Useful when CLEP does not cover the exact slot. DSST is especially helpful for business, technology, social science, and upper-elective style gaps depending on the school.

DSST Fact Sheets →

ACE / NCCRS Providers

Sophia, Study.com, StraighterLine, and similar providers can fill courses that exams miss. They are powerful, but transfer acceptance is school-specific.

John's example: use CLEP for broad general education first, DSST for leftover business or social science slots, and ACE/NCCRS courses only after the target school confirms they land where you need them.
03
Match Every Course to an Exam

Build the credit worksheet.

Now translate the degree map into an execution worksheet. Each row should answer one question: "What is the cheapest confirmed way to fill this requirement?" If you cannot answer that yet, the row stays unapproved.

The matching rule

A match is not just "this exam sounds similar." A real match has four parts:

  1. The requirement name from the degree map.
  2. The credit source and exact course or exam title.
  3. The equivalency your school awards.
  4. Written proof from the catalog, transfer guide, or registrar.

Use exact language

Schools often treat similar-looking credits differently. "Intro to Psychology" may map cleanly to PSYC 101, while "Educational Psychology" may land as an elective. Exact titles, credit levels, and course numbers matter.

If the audit says upper-level, lab required, writing intensive, or resident credit, slow down and verify before choosing an external option.

04
Verify Before You Spend a Dollar

Get the transfer answer in writing.

This is where most people lose time. They take a cheap exam, celebrate too early, and later discover it transferred as the wrong elective or did not count at all. Your standard is written confirmation before payment whenever the answer is not obvious from an official equivalency chart.

The registrar email

Subject: Transfer credit verification for [degree/program] Hi [Registrar/Advisor Name], I am planning my remaining credits for the [exact degree name] under the [catalog year] catalog. Before I register or pay for any external exams/courses, I want to confirm how the following credits would apply to my degree audit: 1. [Provider + exact exam/course title] 2. [Credit recommendation or exam score requirement] 3. [Requirement I believe it satisfies] Can you confirm whether this credit will apply to [specific requirement], whether there is a minimum score/grade, and whether there are any limits on transfer, exam, ACE, DSST, or CLEP credits for my program? Thank you, [Your Name] [Student ID, if enrolled]

What proof counts

  • Best: official school equivalency chart or catalog language that names the exam/course.
  • Strong: email from registrar, transfer office, or assigned academic advisor.
  • Useful: degree audit showing the transfer credit in the correct requirement slot.
  • Weak: forum posts, old Reddit threads, AI answers, or "my friend did this in 2021."

Ask about caps

Some schools limit how many credits can come from CLEP, DSST, ACE, AP, transfer courses, or lower-level sources. A plan that works at 30 credits may fail at 90 credits if you miss the cap.

Ask about level

Lower-level elective credit may not satisfy an upper-level major requirement. When the degree map says 300/400-level, ask directly whether the external source awards upper-level credit.

Ask about residency

Every school has some credits you must complete through them. Usually capstone, cornerstone, or final residency credits cannot be replaced with exams.

Do not ask, "Will this transfer?" Ask, "Will this specific exam/course transfer as this specific requirement in this specific degree under this specific catalog?" Precision is what protects the plan.
05
Stack Smart

The order matters.

A fast degree is not just a pile of credits. It is a controlled sequence. You want the highest-confidence, most transferable credits first, then the custom gap-fillers, then the credits the school requires you to finish in-house.

The smart order

  1. Lock the target degree: school, program, catalog year, total credits, residency credits.
  2. Fill broad gen ed first: CLEP and AP credits usually have the clearest published policies.
  3. Use DSST and ACE for gaps: only after you know the exact requirement they fill.
  4. Handle prerequisites before advanced courses: do not trap yourself by missing sequence rules.
  5. Save school-specific credits for last: capstone, cornerstone, residency, or advisor-required courses.

The bad order

  • Taking random exams because they are easy.
  • Buying ACE courses before checking school-specific partner acceptance.
  • Overfilling free electives while major requirements remain unsolved.
  • Ignoring upper-level requirements until the end.
  • Enrolling too early and paying tuition while you could still stack external credits.

Transcript in batches

Do not send a transcript after every tiny move unless the school requires it. Batch completed credits, then confirm they landed correctly in the audit.

Protect the major

Major requirements are less flexible than general education and electives. Verify major slots first if the degree depends on them.

Leave a buffer

If the degree needs 120 credits, do not build a plan that only works if every credit lands perfectly. Leave room for one transfer surprise.

John's sequencing rule: cheap, obvious, published-credit wins first; ambiguous credits second; school-only credits last. Speed comes from reducing uncertainty, not from rushing the wrong exam.
06
Credit Stacking Cheatsheet

Print this before you start.

Use this checklist every time you add a credit source. The goal is simple: no orphan credits, no surprise electives, no wasted exams.

One-page checklist

I downloaded or saved the official degree map for my catalog year.
I marked every requirement as general education, major, elective, residency, capstone, or locked.
I listed the school limits for CLEP, DSST, AP, ACE/NCCRS, transfer, lower-level, and upper-level credit.
Every planned credit source has an exact exam/course title and expected equivalency.
Every unclear match has written confirmation from an official source.
I am taking broad, high-confidence credits before ambiguous gap-fillers.
I know how to send each transcript or score report to the school.
After each transcript posts, I update the worksheet with where the credit actually landed.

The 90-minute setup

  1. 15 minutes: save the degree map and catalog page.
  2. 20 minutes: highlight locked requirements and transfer caps.
  3. 25 minutes: build the worksheet with every remaining requirement.
  4. 20 minutes: match the obvious CLEP, DSST, AP, ACE, or transfer options.
  5. 10 minutes: email the registrar about every uncertain row.

After that, you do not need more motivation. You need one approved row, one study block, and one completed credit at a time.
