Chapter 03

The Credit Stacking System

Credit stacking is the core strategy. Instead of paying thousands of dollars per course, you earn college credits through alternative pathways — exams, dual enrollment programs, and prior learning assessments — then transfer them to your degree program.

Think of it like arbitrage. The same college credit that costs $1,500+ at a university can be earned for $97 (or free) through a CLEP exam. Same credit. Same transcript entry. Fraction of the cost and time.

The Five Credit Pathways

📝
CLEP Exams
34 exams (College Board, 2025) available. ~$97 each (+ test center fee of ~$20-35) (exam fee reimbursed via Modern States; sitting fee still applies). Score 50+ out of 80 to pass. Earn 3-6 credits per exam. Accepted at 2,900+ schools. Can be taken year-round at 1,800+ test centers.
Highest ROI
📋
DSST Exams
33+ exams from Prometric. Similar to CLEP but covers different subjects. Can retake after 30 days (vs. 3 months for CLEP). Covers areas like ethics, cybersecurity, and technical writing that CLEP doesn't.
Faster Retake
🎯
AP Exams
You don't need to take the AP class to take the AP exam. Self-study is allowed. Score 3-5 to earn credit (varies by school). Covers advanced subjects that CLEP and DSST don't reach.
Self-Study Option
🏫
PSEO / Dual Enrollment
Take college courses while in high school. Often completely free — state covers tuition, fees, and textbooks. Credits count for both HS and college simultaneously. This was my secret weapon.
John's Pick
💼
Prior Learning Assessment
Some schools award credit for work experience, military service, or professional certifications. Great for adult learners and career-changers looking to accelerate their degree.
Adult Learners
💻
ACE Credit Providers
Online platforms like Sophia Learning, Study.com, and Saylor Academy offer ACE-recommended courses that transfer as college credit. Sophia costs ~$99/month with unlimited courses. Study.com is ~$200/month. Saylor is free with a $5 exam fee. Many students earn 60+ credits through these before ever enrolling.
The Secret Weapon

Beyond the Big Five — Methods Most Students Miss

The five pathways above are your core toolkit, but they are not the complete picture. There are several additional acceleration methods that most students either do not know about or dismiss too quickly. Each one can shave months off your timeline and thousands off your bill — and some of them require zero additional effort because you may have already earned the credit without realizing it.

IB (International Baccalaureate) Credit — A Note on Why I Skipped It. My high school offered IB, and I chose not to pursue it. The reason is simple: spending two years on a subject to earn credits that you could earn in a month or a semester through CLEP, DSST, or AP self-study does not align with the speedrunning approach. The IB Diploma Programme is a two-year commitment with its own curriculum, internal assessments, and extended essay requirements — and at the end of it, you earn roughly the same number of credits you could stack in a fraction of the time through exam-based pathways. If your goal is maximum credits in minimum time, IB is the slow lane. However — if you have already earned an IB diploma, do not leave those credits on the table. Depending on the institution, an IB diploma can be worth 15 to 32 college credits. Request your IB transcript from the International Baccalaureate Organization and send it to your university's registrar. Free credits are free credits, regardless of how long they took to earn. Just do not choose the IB path specifically to accelerate — there are far faster options.

Prior Learning Assessment (PLA). This one is built specifically for adults who have been working, serving in the military, or building skills outside a classroom. The concept is straightforward: you build a portfolio documenting college-level learning you gained through professional experience, corporate training programs, military service, or extensive volunteer work. Faculty reviewers evaluate the portfolio against course-level learning outcomes and award credits where your knowledge matches. Thomas Edison State University and Charter Oak State College run the most expansive PLA programs in the country, and students routinely earn 15 to 30 credits through this pathway alone. Research from the Council for Adult and Experiential Learning shows that adult students with 12 or more PLA credits save an average of 9 to 14 months on their degree timeline and between $1,500 and $10,200 in tuition. If you have five or more years of professional experience in any field, PLA should be one of your first moves.

Professional Certifications That Carry College Credit. Here is something most people do not realize: dozens of professional certifications carry formal ACE credit recommendations, meaning they convert directly into college credit at schools that accept ACE. The Google IT Support Professional Certificate, available on Coursera, carries an ACE recommendation for up to 15 college credits. The Google Data Analytics Certificate carries 12 credits. Google Cybersecurity earns 10 credits. CompTIA certifications — A+, Network+, Security+ — also carry ACE credit recommendations. Many of these programs are free or low-cost through Coursera's financial aid, Google's own scholarships, or employer education benefits. The beauty of this pathway is that you earn two things simultaneously: a valuable industry certification that strengthens your resume immediately, and college credits that count toward your degree. For anyone in tech, IT, or data, this is arguably the highest-value pathway in your entire stack.

Heavy Course Loads and Summer/Winter Sessions. This is the simplest acceleration method and it works at every single school, including those that do not accept any alternative credit. The math is straightforward: taking 18 credits per semester instead of the standard 15 gives you 36 credits per year instead of 30, which means you graduate a full year early over a four-year timeline. Add summer courses — even just 6 to 9 credits — and you are earning 42 to 45 credits per year, putting you on track to finish a 120-credit degree in under three years at a traditional institution. Many schools allow up to 21 credits per semester with advisor approval and a minimum GPA. This approach works in combination with everything else: stack your cheap alternative credits first, then accelerate through your remaining university courses with a heavier load.

Combined Bachelor's/Master's (4+1) Programs. Over 200 universities now offer accelerated programs where you begin taking graduate-level courses during your senior year of undergrad, applying those credits to both your bachelor's and master's degrees simultaneously. Typically, 9 to 12 credits of overlap are allowed. When you combine a 4+1 program with credit stacking for the first two years of your bachelor's, the math becomes extraordinary: you could earn both a bachelor's degree and a master's degree in roughly three years total, at a fraction of what most people pay for the bachelor's alone. If graduate school is anywhere in your plans, research whether your target university offers a combined program before you start — it changes your entire planning calculus.

The Stacking Strategy

The key insight is that these pathways stack. You're not limited to one. A strategic combination might look like:

  • Summer before 10th grade: Take 3 CLEP exams (9 credits, exam fee reimbursed via Modern States; ~$20-35 sitting fee per exam)
  • 10th grade: Enroll in 2 AP courses + self-study 2 more AP exams (12-16 credits)
  • 11th grade: Start PSEO/Dual Enrollment + take 4 more CLEP exams (24-30 credits)
  • 12th grade: Continue PSEO + fill remaining gaps with DSST exams
  • Result: Enter college with 60-90+ credits already done. That's 2-3 years of college, completed for almost nothing.

The Philosophy Behind Stacking — Why This Actually Works

Stacking is not just a list of credit sources. It is a mental model built on one core principle: credit arbitrage. The same 3-credit Introductory Psychology course might cost $1,500 at a state university, $525 at a community college, $199 on Study.com, $33 on Sophia (if you complete three courses in a single month at $99), or $5 on Saylor Academy. The credit is identical on your transcript. The knowledge tested is the same. The accreditation behind it is equivalent. The only difference is the price tag — and smart stackers exploit that difference ruthlessly.

The operating principle is simple: fill as many credits as possible from the cheapest sources first, then move up the cost ladder only when you have to. Start with Saylor's $5 exams and any free CLEP vouchers through Modern States. Then move to Sophia's $99 per month unlimited model and burn through as many courses as you can in 30 days. Use Study.com or DSST exams for the subjects those platforms do not cover. Only then do you enroll at a university — and when you do, you arrive with the maximum possible credits already banked, meaning you pay university tuition rates for the absolute minimum number of courses. Every credit you earn before enrollment is money you never have to borrow, interest you never have to pay, and time you never have to spend in a classroom.

This is why stacking compounds so powerfully. Each method covers gaps the others leave. CLEP handles your general education breadth requirements. Sophia and Study.com fill in the electives and prerequisites. DSST covers niche subjects like organizational behavior and technical writing that CLEP does not touch. PSEO and dual enrollment give you upper-division credit with full classroom rigor. PLA converts your work experience into credit without studying at all. And competency-based programs like WGU let you accelerate through whatever remains. No single pathway gets you to the finish line on its own. But layered together in the right sequence — cheapest first, fill gaps second, university for residency last — they turn a $120,000 four-year commitment into an $8,000 twelve-month sprint.

Saylor Academy — The $5 College Credit Nobody Talks About

If you are looking for the absolute cheapest college credit available anywhere, Saylor Academy is it. Saylor offers 33 ACE-recommended courses that are completely free to take — no subscription, no tuition, no hidden fees. The courses cover foundational subjects like Introduction to Psychology, Principles of Management, Introduction to Computer Science, American Government, and many more. You study on your own time using Saylor's open educational resources, and when you are ready, you take a proctored final exam through SmarterProctoring for just $5. Pass the exam and you earn 3 college credits with an ACE recommendation, which means over 1,000 institutions across the country will accept them as transfer credit. Saylor also has direct transfer agreements with more than 60 partner schools.

The math is almost absurd: $5 for a 3-credit course works out to roughly $1.67 per credit hour. Compare that to $350 to $600 per credit at a public university, and you are looking at a 99 percent discount for identical transcript credit. There are a few rules to know: you must wait 14 days between exam attempts, and you get a maximum of three attempts per course. But at $5 per attempt, even a failed first try costs less than a campus parking pass. Saylor is best for budget-conscious students who want to minimize cost above all else, students who are self-directed learners comfortable studying from textbooks and open courseware, and anyone who wants to bank 15 to 30 cheap credits before enrolling at a university. If you stack 10 Saylor courses before enrollment, you have earned 30 credits for $50 total. That is not a rounding error — that is an entire year of college for the price of a dinner.
