DSST, AP & Other Credit Paths

📖 14 min read
🎯 Credit Pathway
Tools in this chapter
DSST CLEP Transferology

DSST (DANTES Subject Standardized Tests)

DSST offers 33+ (DSST/Prometric) exams covering subjects that CLEP doesn't, like Ethics in America, Cybersecurity, Technical Writing, Introduction to Business, and Personal Finance. Key differences from CLEP:

  • Retake policy: Wait only 30 days (vs. 3 months for CLEP)
  • Originally military-focused, but available to all students
  • Some unique subjects not covered by any other exam program

Top 5 DSST Exams to Consider

  1. Principles of Public Speaking — fulfills oral communication requirements
  2. Introduction to Business — broad business fundamentals
  3. Personal Finance — practical knowledge + college credit
  4. Computing and Information Technology — IT/computer science credit
  5. Introduction to World Religions — humanities elective

getcollegecredit.com — Register for DSST exams online or at a test center

AP Exams (Advanced Placement)

Most students think you need to take an AP class to take an AP exam. You don't. Self-study is allowed for every AP exam. You register through the College Board website, pay ~$98 per exam, and take the test in May.

AP scores range from 1-5. Most colleges accept scores of 3+ for credit, though more selective schools may require 4 or 5. Key exams to consider for self-study:

  • AP Human Geography — accessible content, high pass rate
  • AP Environmental Science — lab component is minimal for the exam
  • AP Psychology — significant overlap with CLEP Introductory Psychology
  • AP Microeconomics / Macroeconomics — complements CLEP economics exams
  • AP US History — if you have strong reading comprehension skills

apstudents.collegeboard.org — Register for AP exams (you do NOT need to take the class)

⚠️
Critical Deadline: AP Registration
AP exams happen ONLY in May. Self-study students must contact an AP coordinator by March 15 at the latest. The preferred registration deadline is typically in the fall (October–November) of the prior year. If you miss it, you wait an entire year. Plan 6+ months ahead.

A Word on International Baccalaureate (IB)

My high school offered the IB programme, and I want to be transparent about why I did not choose it. The IB Diploma Programme is a two-year commitment — two years of prescribed curriculum, internal assessments, an extended essay, Theory of Knowledge, and CAS requirements — all to earn roughly the same number of credits you could stack through CLEP, DSST, and AP self-study in a fraction of the time. When I looked at the math, it was clear: spending two years on a subject to earn credits that I could earn in a month or a single semester did not align with my goals. If you are reading this course because you want to speedrun your degree, IB is not the optimal path. The exam-based pathways we covered earlier — CLEP, DSST, AP self-study — give you the same end result (college credit) with dramatically less time investment.

That said, if you have already earned IB credits or an IB diploma, absolutely claim them. Do not leave free credits on the table. Depending on your target school, an IB diploma can be worth 15 to 32 credits. Binghamton University awards up to 32 credits for IB Diploma scores of 30 or higher, and Texas public institutions are required by law to award at least 24 semester hours. Request your IB transcript from the International Baccalaureate Organization, send it to your university's registrar, and verify exactly which credits transfer using Transferology or the school's IB credit policy page. Free credits are free credits, no matter how they were earned.

The bottom line: if someone asks me whether to start the IB programme specifically to accelerate their degree, my answer is no. Use that same two years to stack CLEP and DSST exams and you will come out with more credits, more flexibility, and less stress. But if IB credits are already in your pocket, they are a weapon in your arsenal. Use them.

Which Pathway Should I Use? Decision Guide

Work through these steps in order to find the right credit pathway for your situation:

  1. Step 1: Do you need gen-ed credits? → Start with CLEP (free via Modern States).
  2. Step 2: CLEP doesn't cover the subject? → Check DSST (verify the current exam and sitting fees).
  3. Step 3: Need to stack credits fast (10+ in a month)? → Sophia ($99/mo, complete courses in 3–7 days).
  4. Step 4: Need upper-division or specialized courses? → Study.com (College Saver starts at $95/mo; Pro is $235/mo) or StraighterLine ($99/mo + course fee; bundles available).
  5. Step 5: Still in high school? → PSEO/Dual Enrollment first, it's free.
  6. Step 6: Have work/military experience? → Look into Prior Learning Assessment (Chapter 10).
  7. Step 7: Want the absolute cheapest credits? → Saylor Academy (free open courses with low-cost proctored credit exams).

ACE Credit Providers — The Accelerator Most Students Miss

ACE (American Council on Education) evaluates non-traditional courses and recommends them for college credit. These online platforms let you earn credits at a fraction of the cost and speed of traditional college, and they're accepted at hundreds of schools including WGU, UMPI, and many state universities.

📘
Sophia Learning
~$99/month, unlimited courses. Open-book quizzes, micro-learning modules. Best for general education. Many students complete 3 credits in 3-5 days. A single month can yield 15-20 credits. Transfers to WGU, SNHU, UMPI, Purdue Global, and dozens more.

Note: Sophia limits you to 2 active courses at a time, but you can finish one and immediately start another. Fastest students complete 4–6 courses per month. Budget 1 month of Sophia ($99) for 12–18 credits if you move quickly.

Plans start around $95/month; Pro is higher. ACE-recommended courses with video lessons and proctored exams. Covers both lower and upper-division courses. Broader catalog than many low-cost providers, useful for courses that CLEP and Sophia don't cover. Check the current plan limits before building your degree map.

Gap Filler

Saylor Academy — Ultra-Low-Cost College Credit

Of all the credit sources in this guide, Saylor Academy is the one that makes people do a double take. It offers free open courses, and select courses carry ACE credit recommendations that can be transcripted after a low-cost proctored exam. The exact eligible catalog, exam fee, and partner acceptance list can change, so confirm the current details on Saylor and with your target school before you count those credits in your plan.

The course catalog covers business, computer science, humanities, science, and math. You will find subjects that often map to general education and elective requirements, but transfer is never automatic. The courses are self-paced: no cohorts, no scheduled lectures, and no tuition for the learning materials. You work through the open-source materials at your own speed, then schedule the proctored credit exam when you are ready. Because retake rules and attempt limits can change, check the current course page before you sit for an exam.

The best strategy for Saylor is to use it for subjects where you already have some baseline knowledge and where your target school has confirmed acceptance. Pair Saylor with Sophia and Study.com for maximum coverage: use Saylor for low-cost subjects it covers, fill speed-friendly gaps with Sophia ($99/month if you move quickly), and reach for Study.com when neither of the others has what you need. This layered approach keeps cost down while maintaining momentum across multiple subjects.

Professional Certifications That Double as College Credit

Here is a pathway that almost nobody in the degree-hacking community talks about, and it might be the most strategically powerful option available to you: earning industry certifications that are ACE-recommended for college credit. This means you complete a professional certification — something that goes on your resume and makes you more employable — and it simultaneously counts as college credit at schools that accept ACE recommendations. You get two outcomes from one effort. The Google IT Support Professional Certificate, available on Coursera, is ACE-recommended for up to 15 college credits, equivalent to five full courses. Google Data Analytics earns you 12 credits. Google Cybersecurity earns 10 credits. Google Project Management earns 12 credits. These are not obscure certifications. They are recognized across the tech industry and carry real weight with employers. Many students can access Coursera for free through their local library system or through Coursera's financial aid program, which means the certification itself costs nothing.

Beyond Google's certificates, CompTIA's industry-standard certifications — A+, Network+, and Security+ — are also ACE-recommended for college credit at many institutions. These certifications are considered essential credentials in the IT industry, and passing them demonstrates genuine technical competency. For students pursuing IT, cybersecurity, or business degrees, this is a no-brainer: you are earning a credential that hiring managers actively look for while simultaneously knocking courses off your degree plan. The double benefit is hard to overstate. A traditional student pays $1,500 for a three-credit college course and gets nothing but a transcript line. You pay for a certification exam, get a credential that boosts your starting salary, and earn the same college credits.

The process for transferring certification credit is straightforward. Complete the certification, claim your ACE-endorsed badge through Credly (the digital credentialing platform), and request an official ACE transcript sent to your school. The transcript arrives just like any other transfer credit. However — and this is critical — you must verify with your target school that they accept ACE-recommended credit from these specific certifications before you invest the time. Not every school accepts every ACE recommendation, and policies change. A five-minute phone call to your registrar's office can save you months of wasted effort. Schools like WGU, TESU, Excelsior, and UMPI are generally the most accepting of ACE credit, but always confirm the specifics for your degree program.

Bonus Exam Programs: TECEP & UExcel

Beyond CLEP and DSST, two universities run their own credit-by-exam programs that cover subjects no other exam does:

  • TECEP (Thomas Edison Credit-by-Exam Program) — Run by Thomas Edison State University. Covers subjects like Operations Management, Labor Relations, Abnormal Psychology, and other upper-division courses that CLEP/DSST don't reach. If you're attending TESU, these are essential for filling degree gaps.
  • UExcel (by Excelsior University) — Excelsior's proprietary exams covering Anatomy & Physiology, Pathophysiology, Statistics, and other specialized subjects. Particularly strong for nursing and healthcare pathways. Also accepted at other transfer-friendly schools.

Both programs are especially useful when you've exhausted CLEP and DSST options and need credits in specific upper-division subjects. See Chapter 10 for how these fit into the TESU and Excelsior pathways.

🔥
The Pre-Loading Strategy
The most aggressive credit stackers earn 60-90 credits through Sophia + Study.com + StraighterLine + CLEP before ever enrolling at a university. Then they enter a school like WGU, TESU, Excelsior, or UMPI, transfer everything in, and finish their remaining credits in one term. Total out-of-pocket: roughly $5,000-$6,000 for an entire bachelor's degree. This is the strategy detailed in Chapter 10.
📊
The Pre-Loading Strategy in Action
Real example: A student used Sophia (2 months, $198) for 12 gen-ed credits + CLEP (5 free exams via Modern States) for 15 credits + StraighterLine (1 month, $178) for 4 courses/12 credits = 39 credits for under $400. They then enrolled at WGU and finished in one 6-month term ($3,998). Total bachelor's degree cost: ~$4,400.
Key Takeaway
The most aggressive credit stackers earn 60–90 credits through Sophia, Study.com, CLEP, and StraighterLine before ever enrolling — then transfer everything in and finish for roughly $5,000–$6,000 total.

Identify Your Best Non-CLEP Credits

CLEP isn't your only option. Spend 10 minutes researching which of these pathways fits your situation:

  1. Check your AP scores. If you took AP exams in high school, look up whether your target school accepts them and for how many credits. Don't leave free credits on the table.
  2. Look up DSST exams. Go to getcollegecredit.com and browse the DSST catalog. Are there exams that match courses in your degree plan that CLEP doesn't cover?
  3. Check Sophia and Saylor. These platforms offer self-paced courses accepted by many schools. Search for your degree requirements on each platform.
  4. Add everything to your Credit Planning Tracker with the cost, credit value, and transfer status.

The students who save the most money don't rely on a single credit pathway. They stack all of them.
